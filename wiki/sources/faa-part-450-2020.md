# FAA Part 450 — Streamlined Launch and Reentry License Requirements

**Primary URLs:**
- [Federal Register rule (2020-12-10)](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2020/12/10/2020-22042/streamlined-launch-and-reentry-license-requirements)
- [eCFR Part 450](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-14/chapter-III/subchapter-C/part-450)
- [Cornell LII § 450.161](https://www.law.cornell.edu/cfr/text/14/450.161)
- [FAA Part 450 FAQ](https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/space/additional_information/faq/SLR2_Final_Rule_450.pdf)
- [FAA Part 450 Workshop slides](https://www.faa.gov/sites/faa.gov/files/space/streamlined_licensing_process/Day_2_Part_450_Workshop_Slides_Final.pdf)

**Published:** December 10, 2020 in the Federal Register (85 Fed. Reg. 79566)
**Effective:** March 10, 2021 (180-day compliance transition period)
**Full compliance deadline:** March 10, 2026 (all operators must use Part 450; old parts 415/417/431/435 removed)

## 1. What Part 450 is

Part 450 is a **single, consolidated, performance-based** regulatory framework for all US commercial launch and reentry operations, replacing four legacy Parts:

| Replaced | Covered |
|---------|---------|
| Part 415 | Launch license (large expendable) |
| Part 417 | Launch safety requirements (expendable) |
| Part 431 | Launch and reentry of a reusable launch vehicle |
| Part 435 | Reentry of a reusable launch vehicle |

The consolidation reflects the maturation of the commercial space industry: SpaceX, RocketLab, Blue Origin, and others required a unified framework that could accommodate reusability, novel trajectories, and rapid-cadence launches without bespoke license negotiation.

## 1a. Transition complete + reform in motion (2025–2026, dated)

- **Transition complete (2026-03).** The four legacy Parts (415/417/431/435) were removed and **all** US launch/reentry licensing now runs under Part 450 as of the **March 10, 2026** full-compliance date. Legacy holders transitioned by the **March 9, 2026** deadline (Blue Origin New Shepard, Firefly Aerospace Alpha, SpaceX Falcon 9/Falcon Heavy/Dragon, Rocket Lab Electron, ULA Atlas/Vulcan). The FAA had issued **14 Part 450 licenses** since the rule took effect in March 2021.
- **EO reform mandate (2025-08-13).** The Executive Order *"Enabling Competition in the Commercial Space Industry"* directs the FAA to *"reevaluate, amend, or rescind"* Part 450 requirements — the first political mandate to loosen the framework, seeded by the **SpARC** rulemaking committee (convened Nov 2024, nine topics: flight-safety analysis, system safety, means of compliance, etc.). The EO also orders DOT to *"eliminate or expedite"* NEPA/environmental reviews, the historically dominant delay in the review chain. Net effect for the mission desk: **process time compresses, but the § 450.101 EC ≤ 10⁻⁴ quantitative gate below is statutory and unchanged** — a launch that fails the casualty-expectation analysis still slips regardless of deregulation. See [[sources/faa-ast-launch-licensing-2025]] §2a for the full reform status.

## 2. Scope (§ 450.1)

Part 450 applies to:
- Launches exceeding **150 km altitude**
- Vehicles with total impulse > **200,000 lb-sec**
- Launches of **payload for hire**

It covers pre-flight, flight, and post-flight operations including landing and recovery.

## 3. Safety criteria (§ 450.101) — quantitative limits

The performance-based safety criteria replace prescriptive range safety rules with measurable EC (Expected Casualty) limits:

| Criterion | Limit |
|-----------|-------|
| **Collective risk** to general public | ≤ 1 × 10⁻⁴ expected casualties per operation |
| **Individual risk** — uninvolved person | ≤ 1 × 10⁻⁶ probability of casualty per operation |
| **Aircraft risk** | ≤ 1 × 10⁻⁶ probability of casualty from debris to air traffic |
| **Critical assets** | Protection against high-consequence events (nuclear, radiological, chemical) |
| **On-orbit debris** | Orbiting stage disposal (deorbit or graveyard) within 25 years |
| **Risk to people/property on orbit** | Per-operation analysis required |

The operator proposes and justifies means of compliance. FAA evaluates whether the proposed flight safety analysis demonstrates EC ≤ 10⁻⁴. This is the quantitative gating criterion for license issuance and for each pre-launch flight-commit check.

## 4. Flight safety analysis (§ 450.109 / AC 450.109-1)

Operators must conduct a **Flight Hazard Analysis** documenting:
- Trajectory nominal and dispersed
- Debris hazard footprints (normal flight events + all reasonably foreseeable failure modes)
- Toxic release analysis
- Collision avoidance screening (LCOLA — Launch COnjunction / Collision Avoidance)

The LCOLA screening is the key link to downstream conjunction-avoidance pipelines. SpaceX and TraCSS (NOAA) signed a CRADA in January 2024; SpaceX joined TraCSS as its 10th beta user for LCOLA screenings. As of February 2026, 17 organizations are TraCSS pilot users.

## 5. Hazard area control (§ 450.161) — the NOTAM obligation

**§ 450.161 Control of hazard areas** — full text:

**(a) General:** Operator must publicize, survey, control, or evacuate each flight hazard area identified under § 450.133 before initiating flight, to ensure compliance with § 450.101.

**(b) Verification:** Operator must conduct surveillance to verify and update flight safety analysis assumptions and data.

**(c) Publication:** Operator must publicize warnings for each flight hazard area (except areas under operator/site operator control). If relying on another entity for warnings, operator must:
1. Determine whether warnings have been issued
2. Notify FAA if warnings have not been issued so FAA can determine whether launch/reentry can proceed safely and alert US aircraft

**(d) Application requirements:** Applicants must submit:
1. Day-of-flight surveillance and control procedures
2. Publication methods for flight hazard areas
3. Flight commit criteria from toxic release hazard analysis (§ 450.165(b))

**Practical implication:** The launch operator may rely on the ARTCC to issue the NOTAM and USCG to issue the NOTMAR, but the operator is legally responsible for verifying they were issued before pressing the launch commit button.

## 6. Agreements required (§ 450.147)

Operators must have written agreements establishing procedures for:
- NOTAM issuance (`§ 450.147(a)(3)`)
- USCG NOTMAR issuance (via Letter of Intent / LOI)
- Range safety coordination
- Airspace closure and reopening post-launch

## 7. License duration and modification

- Vehicle Operator License: valid up to **5 years**, renewable
- Mission-Specific License: single operation
- License modifications: processed for new trajectories, annual-launch-limit increases, or new mission profiles (e.g., Starship IFT-9 license modification May 15, 2025: 5 → 25 annual launches; also added new airspace closures over Bahamas/Turks & Caicos)

## 8. Mission-desk notes

- **EC threshold** is the quantitative gate that stops a launch from being attempted — if the flight safety analysis cannot demonstrate EC ≤ 10⁻⁴ for the current window (due to updated atmospheric model, shipping traffic in hazard zone, etc.) the operator must delay. This is a programmatic slip trigger the mission-desk agent can model.
- **LCOLA conjunction screening** is the direct bridge between Part 450 and the orbital conjunction risk pipeline. A failed LCOLA is a regulatory-force slip driver with potentially days-long delay.
- The **25-year deorbit rule** (§ 450.101) affects mission planning for ODC missions: any ODC satellite in LEO must have a confirmed deorbit plan as part of the license application.
